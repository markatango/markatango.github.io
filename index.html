Analysis of MM-L300A Laser Weld Data¶

Stainless steel lap welds¶

Prediction of gap and detection of presence or absence of cover gas¶

Several laser welding experiments were conducted at Amada Miyachi September 19, 2017. Two of these experiments studied the effects of gap between stainless steel (alloy 304) weldment pieces and presence or absence of cover gas (Nitrogen). These experiments are labeled "SS_0_300_N2" and "SS_0_300_NoGas", respectively. The gap distances in the samples range from 0 to 300 microns, as the experiment names imply, and were inferred from post-weld measurements as shown in this figure.

A third experiment, "SS_100", was run with either 100 micron gap or something. An infrared sensor with focusing optics measured relative optical power of infrared emissions in the 1300-2200nm band resulting from each laser spot weld event. The sensor and optics were collinear with the incident laser beam andd focused on the surface of the weldment.

The data from all three experiement are combined in a single DataFrame. Actual gap were inferred from the correlation between distance from edge of the material, assuming linear variation in gap from one end to the other as shown in this figure:

Now we extract features from the waveform data.

First, automatically determine reasonable intervals of interest from the waveform data. To do this, we average all the waveforms, apply a piecewise linear fitter using an mathematical optimizer to set the begin and end points of the intervals. We can choose the maximum number of intervals to generate, and also we can choose a threshold value to eliminate intervals shorter than a prescribed length. These numbers can be used to tune the model, minimize over-fitting, and improve generalization.

For this example, we set a maximum number of intervals at '5', with a minimum acceptable length of '200'.

meanWaveform = np.array(list(data["waveforms"]))
meanWaveform = sum(meanWaveform)/len(meanWaveform)
